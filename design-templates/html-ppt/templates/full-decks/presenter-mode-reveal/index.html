// part 01 · problem

Making slides and giving a talk
are different skills.

✅ Good slides

A coherent theme, clean layout, clear charts, and restrained motion. These define a static artifact.

❌ Good delivery

A clear argument, steady pace, confident answers, and room to adapt.

💡 The difference

The first is work on paper; the second means knowing what to say next after one glance.

Making slides and giving a talk are different skills. A coherent theme, clean layout, and clear charts can be judged offline.

Delivery needs a clear argument, a stable pace, answers to questions, and the ability to adapt to the room.

The key skill is knowing what to say next after one glance at a slide. That comes from a well-designed prompt system, not rote memorization.

// part 02 · presenter view

A presenter view needs four panels

1
Large current slide

Use more than half the view so one glance shows what the audience sees.

2
Next-slide preview

Prepare the transition before you reach the next slide.

3
Speaker notes

Large type, high contrast, and scrolling: the part speakers actually read.

4
Timer + page count

Know elapsed time and remaining slides; pace depends on it.

A presenter view needs four panels. The large current slide is the synchronization anchor between speaker and audience.

The next-slide preview makes transitions natural. The script must be large, high contrast, and scrollable, because the speaker only gets a glance.

The timer and page count keep the pace visible. This deck implements all four panels; press S to try them.

// part 03 · script

Three rules for speaker notes

01
Not a verbatim script: “prompt signals”

Bold core points, isolate transitions, and list data and names — so one glance is enough to continue.

02
150–300 words per slide

Fewer than 150 words is not enough; above 300 is too much to read. Aim for two to three minutes per slide.

03
Write in spoken language, not formal prose

Choose “so” over “therefore.” Read each line aloud; it should sound like speech.

Speaker notes follow three rules. They are not verbatim scripts: bold key points, separate transitions, and list data and names clearly.

Keep each slide between 150 and 300 words. Write in spoken language and read it aloud while drafting.

With these rules, giving a talk becomes more comfortable and more natural.
